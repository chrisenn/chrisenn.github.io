Insurance eDocs

Accessibility and design

Project

Allowing insurance policy holders to log in and download a digital tax receipt, thereby saving costs on printing and mailing out tax receipts, and reducing environmental impact. This project was initiated as a pilot, with a view to going paperless with other documents in the future.

Role

  • UX Design
  • Accessibility
  • QA/Testing

Constraints

  • Had to work within the constraints of the client’s existing database/systems
  • Hard release date

Solution

A repository for online documents (e.g. tax receipt) relating to customers' insurance for easy access, findability and decreased waste for both customer and company. It was also required to include an admin view for employees.

Process

  1. Scoping
  2. Low fidelity prototyping (wireframing and feedback loop)
  3. High Fidelity prototyping (wireframing and feedback loop)
  4. Developer handover
  5. Internal testing
  6. UAT testing with client

Design

Having received a spec from the project manager, I went back and forth with them during which I made some low fidelity prototypes, received feedback and iterated. Once we got it settled, I made my prototype high fidelity. I had intended to use the client's branding and visual style for consistency but I noticed that their current website was a patchwork of different systems with different branding. Hence, I came to decision that I would take the branding, but make the actual interface look different to the marketing website. This was to communicate implicitly that when they log into this new system, it does not carry over to any other part of the client's website – it is standalone, much like the others.

Development

The prototype had received positive feedback from the client and they signed off. However, during development, we encountered some challenges. Although the client had signed off on the proposal, other departments were now getting involved and this caused the (sometimes contradictory) requirements to keep changing over a period of time, which hindered development from advancing. We eventually put a hard stop to it, but even then, it had already taken up a lot of time. The other major challenge we had was that we underestimated how difficult it would be to implement accessibility, particularly screen reader compatibility across different browsers. With our small team, we needed to work across silos if we were going to deliver on time. I helped the developers with the accessibility guidelines. Another designer, the project manager and I took the role of QA and we helped with testing, debugging and helping to find solutions. We also had a feedback loop with the client’s QA to guide us. Through the adversity, we banded together as a team and ended up delivering the product, passing the client’s QA/UAT.

Outcome

The end product was compliant with WCAG AA and it was fully functional with screen readers across different browsers. It received positive feedback from the client who signed on for an additional project.

Lessons Learned

Accessibility

  • Be familiar with all parts of accessibility, even the technical aspects
  • Test with an actual screen reader and allocate a lot of time

Project Management

  • Be wary of scope creep
  • Get everyone in the same room
  • Draw upon existing resources
  • Estimate projects more realistically
